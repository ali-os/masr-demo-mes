Complete MES and OEE Planning Materials Sensor and Excel Parity Design for a Cosmetics Filling and Packing Factory
Executive Summary
A production-grade system for this factory should not be framed as “an OEE dashboard with some forms.” It should be designed as a layered operations platform that joins manufacturing execution, short-horizon planning, BOM-driven material readiness, sensor and PLC ingestion, bilingual loss coding, Excel-parity reporting, and auditable approvals into one governed source of truth. The strongest reference architecture aligns business-to-manufacturing interfaces with models from International Society of Automation[1] ISA-95 and ISA-88, uses industrial interoperability patterns defined by the OPC Foundation[2] and OASIS[3], and applies OT security practices from the National Institute of Standards and Technology[4] and the Cybersecurity and Infrastructure Security Agency[5]. [6]
The uploaded plant materials suggest a practical starting point rather than a blank-sheet exercise: a preparation-to-fill/cap-to-pack pilot flow, machine-level OEE sheets, and a bilingual loss-code library. That means the implementation should preserve historical semantics from existing spreadsheets while moving live truth into governed transactional storage. Excel should remain a seed-data and export format after cutover, not the operational database. fileciteturn0file0
The recommended operating model is departmental and sequential: Planning creates demand and line allocations; Purchasing raises supply intent; Stores reserves and issues ingredients and packaging materials; Ingredients and Preparation transform recipe demand into prepared bulk; Filling and Packing lines execute machine-backed production; Packaging labor completes manual packing stages where applicable; Finished Goods Stores receive achievement; Maintenance owns line and station reliability; Quality owns hold, release, rejection, and rework decisions. The system’s main job is to make every handoff explicit, digitally traceable, and time-bounded. [7]
The most important design choice is to make every data point belong to one of six source types: AUTO_PLC, AUTO_SENSOR, MANUAL, IMPORTED, SYSTEM_CALCULATED, or CORRECTED. Every table, screen, API, alert, and export should carry that provenance, plus approval state, lock state, and audit history. Without that separation, parity with Excel may be possible for a few weeks, but trust in the numbers will eventually collapse. This is especially important where operator downtime coding, reject entry, and backdated corrections interact with machine telemetry. [8]
The strongest technical stack is on-prem first: PLC and sensor inputs land in an edge gateway, are normalized into an event model, are deduplicated and buffered locally for offline conditions, and are forwarded through secure northbound interfaces into the transactional core. OPC UA is the preferred northbound protocol from controllers or industrial PCs because of its information modeling, subscriptions, security model, and audits; MQTT 5 is the preferred lightweight event bus from gateways to the plant application stack because it is publish/subscribe, payload-agnostic, and supports QoS levels that fit telemetry, alerts, and command acknowledgements. Network segmentation, least privilege, and no direct Internet exposure to OT zones should be treated as non-negotiable controls. [9]
The biggest implementation risks are not database design or dashboards. They are five operational failure modes: hidden spreadsheet logic, inconsistent master data, ambiguous downtime codes, missing material readiness checks before line start, and uncaptured waiting time between stations. The report below addresses those directly with a data model, formula registry, department handoff model, line-state semantics, planning controls, Excel reconciliation strategy, and a staged rollout that starts with one pilot line and expands only after formula parity and user-role accountability are proven. [10]
Operating Model and Department Responsibilities
The correct operating model is value-stream based, not software-module based. In this factory, the natural chain is: forecast and target creation → purchasing and supplier acknowledgment → stores receipt and reservation → ingredient issue and preparation → line execution through fill/cap and pack stations → packaging labor and final boxing → finished-goods receipt → historical analysis and continuous improvement. ISA-95’s separation between enterprise planning and manufacturing control is a useful boundary here, while ISA-88’s batch-oriented vocabulary is a good fit for ingredient preparation and formula-driven SKU variation. [11]

The role model should follow the physical flow and the approval burden. Operators should record only immediate operational truth. Supervisors should complete missing context and close operational gaps. Departmental specialists should own the fields that create downstream accountability. Managers should review exceptions and locked-period corrections, not become de facto data entry clerks. That structure reduces both latency and blame-shifting. [12]

Master Data and Information Architecture
The system should treat master data as the long-lived core and events as transient truth around it. That means the order of design should be: products and templates first, BOM and materials second, machine and sensor capability third, line and station topology fourth, plans and work orders fifth, then telemetry and manual events. ISA-88 supports modular product and process definitions; ISA-95 supports the interface between planning and manufacturing execution. [11]

A practical design choice is to keep immutable append-only event tables for telemetry and use explicit correction records rather than editing historical raw events in place. That supports replay, reconciliation, and blame-free retrospectives. Where business users must correct a past truth, the correction should land in a separate table or versioned row linked to the original event. This approach also matches the need for parity tests against historical spreadsheets. [16]
Automation, Events, and Calculation Semantics
The automation architecture should be built around two assumptions: first, the plant will have mixed asset maturity, with some machines exposing rich controller data and some stations needing auxiliary sensors or manual entry; second, replay and conflict resolution are normal, not abnormal. OPC UA is suitable for rich controller and industrial-PC integration because it includes a security model, sessions, subscriptions, information modeling, and auditing. MQTT 5 is suitable for lightweight gateway-to-platform messaging because it decouples producers and consumers and provides QoS choices. Security guidance for OT environments consistently favors segmentation, controlled intermediaries, and preserving safety and reliability over “IT-style” centralization. [17]

The best operator UX is a two-step picker. First select a major family using oversized buttons or tiles with bilingual labels and icons. Second select a short filtered subcode list, defaulted by machine and time of day, with “recent” and “favorites” at the top. If a stop exceeds a configured threshold and no code has been entered, the system should keep the state as “uncoded stop” and escalate it to the supervisor queue rather than silently assigning a generic code. That improves completeness without rewarding bad coding behavior. [19]
Formula engine and Excel parity rules
The formula engine should be explicit, versioned, inspectable, and testable. OEE best practice is to compute Availability from Run Time over Planned Production Time, Performance from Ideal Cycle Time times Total Count over Run Time, and Quality from Good Count over Total Count, then multiply the three factors. The Excel-parity goal is not “use the same cell references”; it is “produce the same business meaning and the same output for the same inputs, with traceable rounding and versioned exceptions.” [20]

Material flow and reservation model
The system should explicitly model the physical handoff chain requested by the user: purchasing acknowledges supply, stores receive and reserve, stores issue ingredients to preparation and packaging materials to line-side use, preparation converts ingredients into prepared bulk, filling and packing consume bulk plus packaging components, final packaging and cartoning complete the product, and finished-goods stores receive achieved output. That chain should be visible in one live plan-vs-materials screen and also reconstructable from stock movements later. ISA-95-style interfaces and BOM-driven requirements make this chain tractable. Deployment, Roadmap, Exceptions, and Self-Validation
The deployment boundary should be on-prem first, with plant services in a segmented OT-aware network zone and carefully controlled upstream connections to business IT. NIST’s OT guidance and CISA’s ICS guidance both emphasize the unique reliability and safety requirements of industrial environments and specifically call out segmentation, controlled access, and the need to avoid exposing critical systems directly. IEC 62443 is the natural longer-term governance framework for the OT security program. [34]

The conclusion is straightforward: the right system is not a dashboard, not a spreadsheet replacement, and not only an MES. It is a controlled plant operating model encoded in software—one that makes departments explicit, turns every handoff into a traceable event, models plans before execution, validates materials before starts, explains calculations after the fact, and uses standards-based automation only where it actually improves reliability and accountability. The design above is implementation-ready because it starts from those operational truths rather than from generic software modules. [35]
